CA Final FR Important IND AS List for Exams

Explanation

As aspiring Chartered Accountants, you're well aware of the vast and intricate syllabus of CA Final Financial Reporting (FR). While every Ind AS is an integral part of the curriculum and can be tested, certain Ind AS consistently feature prominently in examinations due to their complexity, the depth of application required, or their significant impact on financial statements. This "Important Ind AS List" is designed as a strategic guide for your Nov 2026 attempt. It highlights areas that often carry higher weightage, demand more analytical skills, and are frequently combined with other concepts. Please note, this is not a substitute for comprehensive study, but rather a tool to help you prioritize your revision, allocate your study time effectively, and focus on mastering the most frequently tested and challenging standards.

For the Nov 2026 examination, based on historical trends and the evolving nature of financial reporting, the following Ind AS are particularly crucial:

  • Ind AS 103 Business Combinations & Ind AS 110 Consolidated Financial Statements: These standards are foundational for group accounting and often tested together in comprehensive scenarios.
  • Ind AS 116 Leases: A relatively new and complex standard, especially from the lessee's perspective, requiring detailed calculations and application of recognition and measurement principles.
  • Ind AS 109 Financial Instruments: Encompasses recognition, classification, measurement, derecognition, and impairment (ECL model) of financial assets and liabilities. It’s highly conceptual and application-oriented.
  • Ind AS 36 Impairment of Assets: Often tested in conjunction with Ind AS 16 (Property, Plant and Equipment) or Ind AS 38 (Intangible Assets), focusing on recoverable amount, impairment loss calculation, and reversals.
  • Ind AS 38 Intangible Assets & Ind AS 16 Property, Plant and Equipment: Fundamental standards, frequently tested on initial recognition, subsequent measurement, depreciation/amortisation, revaluations, and derecognition.
  • Ind AS 12 Income Taxes: Deals with current and deferred tax, including complex scenarios involving temporary differences, tax holidays, and changes in tax rates.
  • Ind AS 19 Employee Benefits: Specifically the accounting for defined benefit plans, requiring actuarial assumptions and complex disclosures.
  • Ind AS 33 Earnings Per Share: Involves intricate calculations for basic and diluted EPS, especially with convertible instruments, share options, and rights issues.
  • Ind AS 7 Statement of Cash Flows: Application-based, requiring preparation of cash flow statements using direct or indirect method, often involving adjustments for non-cash transactions and foreign currency.

Examples (Exam-Oriented)

Let's look at how some of these important Ind AS are typically tested in the CA Final FR examination:

  • Business Combinations & Consolidation (Ind AS 103 & Ind AS 110): Expect a comprehensive problem involving a parent company acquiring control over a subsidiary. The question will typically require calculating goodwill or capital reserve at the acquisition date, determining the Non-Controlling Interest (NCI) using either the fair value method or proportionate share method, and preparing extracts of consolidated financial statements or specific consolidation adjustments. Scenarios might include inter-company transactions (e.g., unrealised profits on stock or assets), dividend adjustments, and potential changes in ownership interest post-acquisition. The focus is on applying the principles of control, fair value measurement, and eliminating inter-company balances.
  • Leases (Ind AS 116): A common question pattern involves a lessee entering into a lease agreement. You might be required to calculate the Right-of-Use (ROU) asset and lease liability at inception, prepare the amortisation schedule for the lease liability, and show the impact on the lessee's Statement of Profit & Loss and Balance Sheet for several years. Complexities could include lease modifications, reassessment of lease term, variable lease payments, or situations involving purchase options or residual value guarantees. The examiner often tests your ability to identify lease components and non-lease components, and apply the appropriate discount rate.
  • Financial Instruments (Ind AS 109): Questions frequently involve a mix of financial assets and liabilities. You could be asked to classify various instruments (e.g., equity investments, debt instruments, derivatives) based on their contractual cash flow characteristics and the entity's business model. Further, you might need to determine their initial and subsequent measurement (amortised cost, FVOCI, FVTPL) and account for changes in fair value or interest income/expense. The impairment aspect (Expected Credit Loss - ECL model) for debt instruments is also a recurring theme, requiring calculations based on different stages of credit risk.
  • Impairment of Assets (Ind AS 36): A typical problem involves an entity identifying indicators of impairment for a Cash Generating Unit (CGU) or an individual asset. You would need to determine the recoverable amount (higher of Fair Value Less Costs to Sell and Value In Use), calculate the impairment loss, and allocate it, especially within a CGU. Subsequent reversals of impairment are also frequently tested, ensuring you understand the limitations and accounting treatment. Often, this is integrated with other asset-related standards like Ind AS 16 or Ind AS 38, where assets might have revalued amounts prior to impairment.

This list helps you prioritize for effective revision and deeper mastery of frequently tested topics, but it doesn't guarantee passing if you completely ignore other standards. A balanced approach with a strong conceptual understanding of all Ind AS is always recommended.
  • Question: How should I approach the study of these important IND AS for the Nov 2026 exam?
    Answer: For these key Ind AS, focus on a multi-pronged approach: 1. Understand the core principles and recognition criteria. 2. Practice a variety of complex problems from your study material and question bank. 3. Pay close attention to presentation and disclosure requirements. 4. Identify common pitfalls and tricky adjustments. Regular revision of concepts and continuous problem-solving are key.
  • Question: How frequently do the ICAI exam patterns and important IND AS lists change?
    Answer: While the core principles of Ind AS remain stable, the ICAI's testing methodology and the specific focus areas can evolve with each attempt. Major changes to the "important list" are less frequent unless there are significant amendments to the Ind AS themselves or a change in the overall syllabus. However, the *application* of these standards in complex scenarios is constantly refined. Staying updated with the latest RTPs and MTPs is vital.
  • Question: What is the typical weightage of these identified important IND AS in the FR paper?
    Answer: Individually, Ind AS 103/110 and Ind AS 116 can carry significant weight (e.g., 10-15 marks each in a comprehensive question). Other standards like Ind AS 109, Ind AS 36, Ind AS 12 often appear for 5-8 marks. Collectively, these identified important Ind AS often account for 60-70% of the total marks in the FR paper, either as standalone questions or integrated into larger problems.
  • Question: Should I prioritize understanding concepts or solving problems for these IND AS?
    Answer: For CA Final FR, both are equally critical and intertwined. You cannot effectively solve complex problems without a deep conceptual understanding, and your conceptual clarity will be solidified by applying it to diverse problems. Start with conceptual clarity, then immediately reinforce it with problem-solving. Practice writing down the solutions step-by-step to improve presentation and speed.
